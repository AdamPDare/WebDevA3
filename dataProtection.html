    Data Protection Act 1984, 1998 and 2000

    The data protection act was designed to protect the privacy and integrity of all the personal data and information stored upon any computer or any paper filing systems by an organisation. This data associated with an individual must be able to accessible to them and if they need to correct it, they can do so.

    The 8 principles in the DPA

    1. Personal Data must be processed fairly and lawfully. Individuals must be informed upon the purpose of why the data is being collected and whether the information will be sent out of the European Economic Area.

    2. Data can only be used for the purposes for which it was collected. The data being collected can only be used for one purpose; these purposes must be legal and reasonable

    3. The data collected must be relevant, adequate and must not be excessive

    4. The data must be accurate and kept up to date

    5. Data cannot be held for longer than needed A retention policy could be enforced to extend the duration of which the data is kept for.

    6. The data must be processed according to the rights of the individual Provide individuals with a version of the data that has been collected upon them. This must be done within 40 days if the user has requested to view this.

    7. Technical and organisational measures need to be put in place to protect the confidentiality of the data. Protect the data from hackers/ malicious software and unauthorised access.

    8. Data cannot be transferred out of the European Economic Area unless it is accurate, and measures have been put in place to protect it.
